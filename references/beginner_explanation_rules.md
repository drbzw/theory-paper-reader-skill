# Beginner-Oriented Explanation Rules

## 1. Audience

Write for a strong empirical finance researcher who understands identification, regression design, institutional settings, and causal mechanisms but has not received systematic training in formal theory.

Do not assume the reader already knows why IR, IC, single crossing, continuation utility, posterior beliefs, or residual control rights matter.

## 2. Explanation order

Use this order unless the paper requires a different one:

1. the real economic problem;
2. the friction-free benchmark;
3. the friction;
4. who chooses what and when;
5. the trade-off created by the friction;
6. the equilibrium response;
7. the non-obvious result;
8. comparative statics;
9. welfare and distribution;
10. empirical translation.

Economic intuition should precede notation.

## 3. Equations

For each load-bearing equation, explain three things in prose:

- what the equation means economically;
- why the model needs it;
- what would change if it were removed or altered.

Do not reproduce every derivation. Focus on the equations that create the model's tension or determine the result.

## 4. Assumptions

Distinguish **load-bearing assumptions** from technical conveniences. A load-bearing assumption changes the direction or existence of the main result. A technical convenience makes the proof easier without defining the economic mechanism.

State the strongest likely objection a theory-trained reader would raise.

## 5. Results

Separate:

- results written directly into assumptions;
- mechanical optimization consequences;
- genuinely non-trivial equilibrium results;
- interpretation that goes beyond what was formally shown.

## 6. Style

Use numbered sections and subsections. Write coherent paragraphs with several related sentences. Use bold text sparingly to emphasize the central conclusion or distinction. Avoid one-sentence paragraphs, excessive bullets, glossary-style fragments, and “Notion-like” cards.

## 7. Empirical bridge

Explain how the model changes empirical reasoning. Identify the latent primitive, plausible proxy, shock that could move it, shortest response time, maximum plausible lag, predicted outcome, and competing models that could generate the same average effect.
