# Model-Family Classification Rules

## 1. Classification is structural, not lexical

Do not classify from titles, abstracts, JEL codes, or isolated words such as “signal,” “agency,” or “information.” Recover the model's structure first: agents, timing, information, actions, constraints, commitment, equilibrium concept, and the friction that blocks first-best.

## 2. Use a hierarchy

Every classification should distinguish:

1. **Primary family** — the model's main skeleton.
2. **Specific subfamily** — the narrowest established version that fits.
3. **Secondary or extension family** — the source of an added mechanism.
4. **Application domain** — the real-world setting, which is not itself a model family.

Example: `Primary: moral hazard; Subfamily: multitask agency; Extension: career concerns; Domain: ESG compensation.`

## 3. Open-set rule

The local seed map is not exhaustive. A paper may belong to a known family absent from the repository, combine multiple families, or remain unresolved. Never force a paper into the nearest available label merely to complete the task.

## 4. Core structural fingerprints

### 4.1 Signaling

The informed party moves first and takes a costly or otherwise type-dependent observable action. An uninformed receiver updates beliefs and responds. Ask whether the action is chosen partly to influence beliefs.

### 4.2 Screening and adverse selection

The agent has private type information before contracting. An uninformed principal offers a menu or mechanism, and types self-select. Incentive compatibility across types is load-bearing.

### 4.3 Moral hazard

A contract or institutional arrangement is chosen before an action that is unobservable or imperfectly observable. Outcomes are noisy signals of action. The main trade-off usually involves incentives, risk sharing, limited liability, or information quality.

### 4.4 Career concerns

Current actions affect outsiders' beliefs about persistent ability or type, changing future wages, authority, or opportunities. Even without an explicit incentive contract, reputational incentives shape behavior.

### 4.5 Incomplete contracts and property rights

Future actions or states cannot be fully specified or enforced. Ownership allocates residual control rights and thereby changes ex ante non-contractible investment incentives. Do not classify every incomplete written agreement as an incomplete-contract model.

### 4.6 Authority and delegation

The core allocation is decision rights rather than ownership. Centralization trades goal alignment and control against the use of local information or initiative. Distinguish formal authority from real authority.

### 4.7 Financial contracting and security design

The model endogenizes cash-flow rights, control rights, repayment, liquidation, collateral, covenants, or staged finance to address a financing friction. Identify the specific baseline friction rather than using “financial contracting” as a terminal label.

### 4.8 Disclosure and voluntary revelation

An informed sender chooses whether, when, or how much verifiable information to disclose, often under disclosure costs or proprietary costs. Distinguish disclosure from cheap talk and persuasion.

### 4.9 Bayesian persuasion and information design

A sender commits ex ante to an information structure or signal experiment that shapes posterior beliefs. The object chosen is the information structure itself, not merely a message after learning the state.

### 4.10 Cheap talk and strategic communication

Messages are non-verifiable and typically costless. Informativeness depends on preference alignment, message space, and equilibrium beliefs.

### 4.11 Costly information acquisition

Agents choose whether or how much information to acquire and pay a direct cost. The information technology may be discrete or continuous.

### 4.12 Rational inattention

Information processing is constrained by capacity or an information-theoretic cost. Do not use this label whenever attention is merely costly; verify the formal information-cost structure.

### 4.13 Internal capital markets

A headquarters or controlling entity reallocates resources across divisions using internal information and authority. The central tension often pits winner-picking or financing advantages against influence activities, favoritism, or information loss.

### 4.14 Relational contracts

Non-verifiable actions are supported by the continuation value of repeated interaction rather than court enforcement. Identify the self-enforcement condition and punishment path.

## 5. Candidate adjudication

For each serious candidate, answer:

- Which structural elements match?
- Which element is missing or contradictory?
- Is it the baseline skeleton or only an extension?
- What observable condition would make the classification collapse into another family?

Always reject the two strongest alternatives explicitly. The purpose is pedagogical as well as classificatory.

## 6. Relabeling test

A paper is at high risk of superficial relabeling when the setting and variable names change but agents, timing, information, constraints, equilibrium conditions, and central result map one-to-one onto the ancestor. A new application may still be empirically important, but it is not a new model family.
